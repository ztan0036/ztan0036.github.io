For the majority of residents in Melbourne's central business district, the streets, parks, cafes, bars, and graffiti strewn throughout the city's back streets and alleys are the city's most appealing features.
However, do you truly understand the ground beneath your feet?
According to historical records, Melbourne was dormant for a time, and its current title of "World's Most Livable City" is the result of more than two decades of urban space adjustment efforts. It has witnessed the transformation of Melbourne's inner city, particularly the CBD area, from near abandonment to rebirth.
When it comes to Melbourne's city center, the fact that there is no decent public open space within the "Hoddle Road Network," as it is commonly known, is often overlooked. Flagpole Park, in the north-west of the city center, was established 38 years after Melbourne was founded as Australia's first public open green space. Flagpole Park was conceived as a forerunner to the cemetery where the first colonists were entombed. Several other major parks, such as the "Parliament Park" surrounding the Victorian State Parliament Building and the "Treasury Park" surrounding the original Victorian State Treasury Building, as well as those redesigned for the 1880 Melbourne World Expo, are primarily reserved for public use and have no private ownership. In Melbourne's city center, for a long time, the public open spaces were little more than a collection of industrialized streets. As Melbourne grows from a small town to Australia's second largest city as a result of industrialization, the streets of Melbourne have nothing to do with comfort or convenience.
This was not the result of a clerical error in urban planning, but rather a deliberate decision on the part of the colonial authorities. Initially, the colonists established themselves on the Australian continent primarily for economic purposes. The city's road network was created with the goal of quickly establishing a land market and selling development plots to private individuals at a profit. The colonists were concerned that public spaces, such as squares, would attract people with a wide range of demands, posing a threat to their ability to maintain their rule. According to a government official at the time, "the square will only serve to strengthen democratic principles." At the time of Melbourne's founding 180 years ago, "democracy" was not among the political options available to colonial officials dispatched by the British Empire to establish the colony.
